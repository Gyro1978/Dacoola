WhatsApp Spyware Lawsuit Outcome: $167M Verdict and Critical Takeaways

A jury has ordered NSO Group to pay WhatsApp over $167 million in damages, marking a significant conclusion to a five-year legal battle over the misuse of Pegasus spyware. The WhatsApp spyware lawsuit outcome underscores the growing legal consequences for companies exploiting encrypted messaging vulnerabilities. The case revealed startling details about NSO Group’s operations, including its targeting of 1,400 WhatsApp users through a zero-click attack in 2019.

How NSO Group Exploited WhatsApp’s Infrastructure

The lawsuit exposed the mechanics of NSO Group’s zero-click attack, which required no user interaction. WhatsApp’s legal team demonstrated that the spyware maker deployed a fake "WhatsApp Installation Server" to send malicious messages mimicking legitimate communications. These messages triggered targets’ phones to download Pegasus spyware, exploiting a flaw in WhatsApp’s audio-calling feature. Tamir Gazneli, NSO Group’s R&D VP, admitted that such zero-click exploits represented a "significant milestone" for Pegasus.

The case also highlighted the broader implications of cyber surveillance lawsuits, as NSO Group continued targeting WhatsApp users even after the lawsuit was filed in late 2019. Codenamed "Hummingbird," the attack variants "Erised," "Eden," and "Heaven" remained active until mid-2020.

NSO Group’s Admission: Targeting a U.S. Number for the FBI

Contrary to its claims, NSO Group confirmed it tested Pegasus on a U.S. phone number during an FBI demonstration. While the agency chose not to deploy the spyware, this revelation challenges NSO’s longstanding denial of targeting +1 country codes. The incident raises questions about the ethical boundaries of government hacking tools and their potential misuse.

The Global Reach of Pegasus Spyware

NSO Group CEO Yaron Shohat testified that Pegasus’s backend automatically selects exploits for government clients, prioritizing intelligence gathering over method transparency. The lawsuit disclosed three government customers-Mexico, Saudi Arabia, and Uzbekistan-and revealed NSO had severed ties with 10 clients for abuse. This underscores the risks of unchecked cyber espionage and the need for stronger cybersecurity legal precedents.
