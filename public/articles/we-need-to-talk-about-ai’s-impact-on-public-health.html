We Need to Talk About AI’s Impact on Public Health

The rapid growth of AI is driving a surge in data center energy and water consumption, with U.S. electricity demand projected to reach 6.7–12% of the national total by 2028. However, the AI environmental impact goes beyond resource use—backup generators and fossil-fueled power plants emit harmful pollutants like PM2.5 and nitrogen oxides, linked to asthma, heart disease, and cognitive decline. Research estimates U.S. data centers caused $6 billion in public health damages in 2023, a figure that could rise to $20 billion by 2030.

Why It Matters

While AI’s carbon footprint is widely discussed, its health consequences remain overlooked. Air pollution is a "silent killer," responsible for 4 million premature deaths annually. Unlike carbon emissions, health risks vary by location, meaning efficiency gains alone won’t solve the problem. Demand-side strategies, like routing AI workloads to cleaner regions, could mitigate harm without major infrastructure changes. As AI becomes a public utility, integrating health considerations into its deployment is critical to balancing innovation with public well-being.
