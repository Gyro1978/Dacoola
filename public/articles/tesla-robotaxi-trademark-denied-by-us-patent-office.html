Tesla Robotaxi Trademark Denied by U.S. Patent Office

Tesla’s attempt to trademark the term "Robotaxi" for its autonomous vehicle service has been rejected by the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office. Officials ruled the term too generic, dealing a setback to Tesla’s branding strategy for its self-driving taxi ambitions. Meanwhile, the company’s "Cybercab" trademark applications were also halted due to competing claims on similar "Cyber" trademarks.

Trademark Rejection and Tesla’s Autonomous Plans

The denial of Tesla’s "Robotaxi" trademark highlights regulatory challenges in branding autonomous vehicle services. The U.S. Patent Office determined the term lacked distinctiveness, as it broadly describes a type of service rather than a unique product. This decision complicates Tesla’s efforts to establish a recognizable identity for its driverless taxi initiative.

Tesla’s "Cybercab" applications faced a different obstacle-multiple companies have already filed for trademarks containing "Cyber," creating potential conflicts. While Tesla can appeal or rebrand, the delays may slow its go-to-market strategy. The company has not yet commented on next steps.

Broader Industry Developments

Beyond Tesla, the mobility sector saw significant shifts this week. Aurora Innovation, a self-driving truck company, lost co-founder Sterling Anderson, who stepped down as chief product officer. Aurora plans to expand its driverless truck operations despite the leadership change.

Meanwhile, Uber deepened its investments in autonomous technology, partnering with Chinese firms Pony AI, Momenta, and WeRide to expand robotaxi services in Europe and the Middle East. Waymo also made headlines, revealing plans to scale production of its autonomous Jaguar I-Pace vehicles.
