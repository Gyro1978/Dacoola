Google Signs Deal for 1.8 GW of Advanced Nuclear Power

Google has partnered with nuclear developer Elementl Power to build three advanced nuclear reactor sites, aiming to add at least 1.8 gigawatts of power capacity. The move comes as the company accelerates its AI infrastructure, with plans to spend $75 billion on data centers this year alone.

Under the agreement, each site will generate a minimum of 600 megawatts, with Google securing the option to purchase power directly from the grid. Elementl, backed by Breakwater North and Energy Impact Partners, has kept a low profile until now but brings nuclear industry expertise to the project.

Key Details and Industry Context

The reactors will likely use small modular reactor (SMR) technology, though Elementl remains "technology agnostic" and has not yet selected a specific vendor. Kairos Power, which already has a partnership with Google, is a strong contender. Its demonstration plant produces 50 megawatts, with commercial units expected to reach 150 megawatts.

SMRs typically cap at around 300 megawatts-far smaller than traditional plants like Georgia’s Vogtle Unit 4, which generates 1.1 gigawatts. However, their modular design allows for faster deployment and potential cost savings through mass production.

Silicon Valley has shown growing interest in SMRs, with startups like Oklo and X-Energy also securing deals. Despite enthusiasm, no SMR projects have been completed outside China. NuScale, a U.S. startup, faced setbacks last year when costs ballooned and a key contract was canceled.

Pros & Cons

Pros
  • Provides *consistent* carbon-free energy for Google’s AI-driven data centers.
  • SMRs offer scalability and potential cost reductions through standardized manufacturing.
Cons
  • Unproven commercial viability of SMRs outside China raises execution risks.
  • Regulatory hurdles and public skepticism could delay project timelines.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Google investing in nuclear power?

Google’s AI expansion demands massive energy, and nuclear offers reliable, low-carbon electricity to meet those needs.

What makes SMRs different from traditional reactors?

SMRs are smaller, modular, and designed for faster deployment, with potential cost savings from standardized production.

Has any SMR been built in the U.S.?

No. NuScale’s project was canceled in 2023 due to rising costs, leaving China as the only country with operational SMRs.
