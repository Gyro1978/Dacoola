Pegasus Spyware Ruling Orders NSO to Pay $167M for WhatsApp Hack

The Israeli firm NSO Group, creator of the Pegasus spyware, has been ordered to pay WhatsApp $167 million (£125 million) for hacking 1,400 users in 2019. This marks the first time a spyware developer has been held accountable for exploiting smartphone vulnerabilities. Pegasus, which can remotely access phone microphones and cameras, has been linked to surveillance of journalists, activists, and political figures.

Meta, WhatsApp's parent company, called the ruling a "first victory against illegal spyware," while NSO stated it would review the verdict and consider an appeal. The case followed a six-year legal battle between the tech giant and the surveillance firm.

Legal and Ethical Implications

The Pegasus spyware ruling sets a precedent for holding spyware developers legally responsible for misuse. NSO claims its technology targets criminals and terrorists, but reports suggest authoritarian regimes have used it against dissidents and journalists. A 2021 leak revealed 50,000 phone numbers of potential victims, including French President Emmanuel Macron and associates of murdered Saudi journalist Jamal Khashoggi.

WhatsApp's victory could encourage other tech companies to pursue legal action against NSO. The firm must also pay Meta an additional $444,000 in damages. Legal experts say the decision may deter similar surveillance abuses, though NSO maintains its tools are used responsibly by government agencies.

Global Impact and Reactions

Pegasus has been tied to breaches in multiple countries, including suspected infections on devices belonging to UK government officials. Canadian watchdog The Citizen Lab has extensively documented its misuse. Meta emphasized that the ruling serves as a deterrent against illegal surveillance targeting US companies.
