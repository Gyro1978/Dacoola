Tesla Denied "Robotaxi" Trademark as USPTO Deems Term Too Generic

Tesla's attempt to trademark the term "Robotaxi" for its autonomous vehicles has been rejected by the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO). The agency ruled the term is too generic to qualify for trademark protection. Separately, Tesla's "Cybercab" trademark applications were also halted due to existing "Cyber" trademarks held by other companies.

Trademark Challenges and Tesla's Autonomous Strategy

The USPTO's decision highlights the difficulties companies face when trying to trademark widely used terms. "Robotaxi" is considered descriptive rather than distinctive, making it ineligible for exclusive branding rights. Tesla has not yet commented on whether it will appeal the decision or rebrand its autonomous ride-hailing service.

Meanwhile, Tesla's "Cybercab" filings were paused because multiple companies already hold trademarks incorporating "Cyber." This could delay Tesla's branding efforts for its upcoming autonomous taxi fleet, which CEO Elon Musk has repeatedly promoted as a key part of the company's future.

Broader Mobility Industry Updates

Aurora Innovation, a competitor in autonomous trucking, saw co-founder Sterling Anderson resign unexpectedly. Anderson, who served as chief product officer, cited an "exciting external opportunity" but did not disclose specifics. Aurora confirmed plans to expand its driverless trucking operations to Houston, El Paso, and Phoenix by late 2025.

In other developments, Uber deepened its investments in autonomous driving, increasing funding for China's WeRide and acquiring a majority stake in Trendyol Go, a Turkish delivery service. Waymo also announced a new factory in Arizona to scale production of its autonomous Jaguar I-Pace fleet.
