Florida Encryption Backdoors Bill Fails After Legislative Push

A Florida bill that would have forced social media companies to create encryption backdoors for law enforcement access has failed to pass. The legislation, known as the Social Media Use by Minors bill, was withdrawn from consideration in the Florida House of Representatives after the Senate had already advanced it. The proposal would have required tech firms to decrypt user messages when served with a subpoena, raising concerns among digital rights advocates.

Legislative Hurdles and Security Concerns

The bill faced strong opposition from privacy and security experts, who argued that encryption backdoors inherently weaken user protections. The Electronic Frontier Foundation labeled the proposal "dangerous and dumb," citing the impossibility of creating a secure backdoor without exposing data to potential breaches. Law enforcement agencies typically issue subpoenas without judicial oversight, which could have led to unchecked access to private communications.

Security professionals have long warned that any mechanism allowing decryption could be exploited by malicious actors. The bill's failure reflects growing recognition of these risks, even as some lawmakers push for expanded surveillance powers. Florida's legislative process requires both chambers to approve a bill before it becomes law, and the House's decision to withdraw it effectively ended its progress.
