WhatsApp Scores $167M Victory in Landmark NSO Group Spyware Lawsuit

A California jury ordered NSO Group to pay WhatsApp over $167 million in damages, concluding a five-year legal battle over the spyware maker’s exploitation of a vulnerability in the messaging app’s audio-calling feature. The lawsuit, filed in 2019, accused NSO Group of hacking 1,400 WhatsApp users via zero-click attacks-exploits requiring no user interaction.

The verdict follows explosive testimonies revealing NSO Group’s internal operations, including its testing of Pegasus spyware on a U.S. phone number for the FBI and continued targeting of WhatsApp users even after the lawsuit was filed.

Key Revelations from the Trial

Zero-Click Attack Mechanics: WhatsApp’s lawyer Antonio Perez detailed how NSO Group’s “WhatsApp Installation Server” sent malicious messages mimicking legitimate traffic. These triggered devices to download Pegasus spyware-requiring only a phone number. NSO’s VP Tamir Gazneli admitted zero-click exploits like “Erised” were operational until mid-2020.

U.S. Number Targeting Confirmed: Despite longstanding denials, NSO’s lawyer acknowledged a “single exception” where Pegasus was configured to demonstrate capabilities to the FBI. The agency ultimately declined deployment.

Government Customer Oversight: CEO Yaron Shohat testified that Pegasus automatically selects hacking methods for clients, prioritizing intelligence over technical specifics. Notably, NSO had severed ties with 10 governments for abusing the spyware.

Broader Implications

The case exposed locations of 1,223 victims and implicated Mexico, Saudi Arabia, and Uzbekistan as NSO customers. It also highlighted the spyware industry’s opacity-contrasting NSO’s open headquarters (shared with Apple) with rivals like Variston, which concealed operations.
