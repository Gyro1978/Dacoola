OpenAI Aims to Slash Revenue Share Paid to Microsoft by 2030

OpenAI expects to significantly reduce the revenue share it pays to Microsoft by the end of the decade, according to a report by The Information. Currently, OpenAI shares 20% of its revenue with Microsoft under their existing agreement, but internal projections suggest this could drop to 10% by 2030. This shift aligns with OpenAI’s broader restructuring plan, which includes transitioning its for-profit arm into a public benefit corporation (PBC) while remaining under nonprofit oversight.

Microsoft, which has invested tens of billions into OpenAI, holds exclusive rights to OpenAI’s APIs on Azure and access to its intellectual property. However, the tech giant has yet to approve OpenAI’s proposed corporate restructuring, reportedly seeking assurances that its massive investment remains protected.

Unpacking the Financial and Strategic Implications

The reduction in revenue-sharing reflects OpenAI’s growing financial independence and confidence in its long-term profitability. Currently, Microsoft benefits from OpenAI’s AI advancements, integrating them into products like Azure and Copilot. A lower revenue share could indicate OpenAI’s intent to diversify partnerships or renegotiate terms as its valuation grows.

This move also raises questions about Microsoft’s future leverage over OpenAI. While the current contract runs until 2030, a revised revenue split may prompt renegotiations on exclusivity clauses, potentially opening OpenAI’s technology to other cloud providers.

Corporate Restructuring and Investor Concerns

OpenAI’s shift to a public benefit corporation model aims to balance profit motives with its original mission of ensuring AI benefits humanity. However, Microsoft’s hesitation suggests concerns over governance and financial safeguards. If approved, the new structure could redefine how OpenAI allocates profits while maintaining its nonprofit oversight.
