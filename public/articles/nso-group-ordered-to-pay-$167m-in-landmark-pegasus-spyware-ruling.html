NSO Group Ordered to Pay $167M in Landmark Pegasus Spyware Ruling

The Israeli firm NSO Group, creator of the controversial Pegasus spyware, has been ordered to pay WhatsApp $167 million (£125 million) for hacking 1,400 users in 2019. The ruling marks the first time a spyware developer has been held accountable for exploiting smartphone vulnerabilities.

Pegasus, a powerful surveillance tool, can remotely infiltrate mobile devices to access microphones, cameras, and sensitive data. NSO Group claims its technology is intended solely for tracking criminals and terrorists, but critics argue it has been misused by authoritarian regimes to target journalists, activists, and political figures.

Legal and Ethical Implications

The verdict follows a six-year legal battle between Meta, WhatsApp’s parent company, and NSO Group. Meta hailed the decision as a "first victory against illegal spyware," while NSO stated it would review the ruling and consider further legal action. The case sets a precedent, potentially exposing NSO to lawsuits from other tech firms affected by Pegasus.

In 2021, leaked documents revealed Pegasus had been used against high-profile individuals, including French President Emmanuel Macron and associates of murdered journalist Jamal Khashoggi. The spyware also allegedly targeted UK government officials, according to investigations by The Citizen Lab.

Broader Impact on Surveillance Industry

The ruling could deter companies from developing intrusive spyware, but NSO maintains its tools are vital for national security. The firm must also pay Meta an additional $444,000 in damages.
