Why the humanoid workforce is running late

Despite investor enthusiasm and bold predictions of a billion humanoid robots by 2050, experts like MIT’s Daniela Rus argue that current humanoid robotics progress falls short of practical deployment. Rus highlighted fundamental issues, such as robots lacking common sense and struggling with basic tasks, while Agility Robotics’ CTO Pras Velagapudi pointed to power and safety constraints. Even high-profile startups like Figure AI face skepticism, with BMW reportedly scaling back its partnership despite the company’s claims of rapid progress.

Why It Matters

The gap between hype and reality in humanoid robotics underscores deeper challenges in AI and mechanical engineering. While investors push for rapid adoption, roboticists emphasize that real-world deployment requires solving safety, efficiency, and adaptability hurdles—problems that take years, not months, to address. This disconnect suggests that, unlike software-based AI, physical robotics will evolve incrementally, with adoption varying widely across industries. The current cycle of flashy demos and inflated expectations risks diverting attention from the foundational work needed for sustainable progress.
