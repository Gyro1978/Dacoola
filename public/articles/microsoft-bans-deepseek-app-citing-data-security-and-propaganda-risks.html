Microsoft bans DeepSeek app citing data security and propaganda risks

Microsoft has prohibited its employees from using the DeepSeek app, citing concerns over data security and potential exposure to Chinese propaganda. The company's president, Brad Smith, confirmed the ban during a Senate hearing, stating that DeepSeek's data storage practices and content moderation policies pose significant risks.

DeepSeek's privacy policy confirms that user data is stored on servers in China, making it subject to local laws requiring cooperation with intelligence agencies. Additionally, the AI chatbot heavily censors topics deemed sensitive by the Chinese government. While Microsoft has restricted internal use, it still offers DeepSeek's R1 model on Azure, though modified to mitigate "harmful side effects."

Key concerns behind the DeepSeek ban

Microsoft's decision stems from fears that employee data could be stored in China and that DeepSeek's responses may reflect state-influenced narratives. Smith emphasized that while the company allows third-party AI models on Azure after rigorous safety checks, the standalone DeepSeek app presents unique risks.

Despite the ban, Microsoft has not removed all competing AI chatbots from its Windows app store. For example, Perplexity remains available, while Google's Gemini and Chrome browser are notably absent. The distinction highlights Microsoft's selective approach to AI regulation based on perceived security threats.

Microsoft's modifications to DeepSeek

During the Senate hearing, Smith revealed that Microsoft altered DeepSeek's AI model to reduce risks before offering it on Azure. The company conducted extensive safety evaluations but did not disclose specific changes. This move suggests Microsoft sees value in DeepSeek's technology while mitigating its potential dangers.
