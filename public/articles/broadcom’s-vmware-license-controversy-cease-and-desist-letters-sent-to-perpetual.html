Published on May 07, 2025 by AI News Team   |   View Original Source
<h2>Broadcom’s VMware License Controversy: Cease-and-Desist Letters Sent to Perpetual Users</h2> <p>Broadcom has intensified the <strong>VMware license controversy</strong> by issuing cease-and-desist letters to users holding VMware perpetual licenses with expired support contracts. Following its acquisition of VMware in November 2023, Broadcom discontinued perpetual license sales, forcing users to either transition to costly subscription bundles—reportedly up to 300% more expensive—or operate without support. Now, unsupported users are receiving legal warnings demanding they stop using updates released after their contracts expired, even if they never installed them. </p> <h3>Unpacking Broadcom’s Aggressive Enforcement</h3> <p>Broadcom’s cease-and-desist letters, signed by managing director Michael Brown, explicitly prohibit the use of any VMware updates—including patches, bug fixes, or security enhancements—beyond the expiration date of a user’s support contract. The letters frame continued use as a "material breach" and an infringement of VMware’s intellectual property, threatening legal action for damages and attorneys’ fees. </p> <p>Reports indicate that some recipients, such as clients of Canadian MSP Members IT Group, received these warnings just days after their contracts lapsed, despite not having installed updates. Even users who migrated to alternatives like Proxmox have been targeted, raising questions about Broadcom’s enforcement strategy. </p> <h4>The Fallout for VMware Customers</h4> <p>The move has left many VMware users scrambling for alternatives, with some opting for competitors like Nutanix or considering <strong>devirtualization</strong>—a costly and complex process.
