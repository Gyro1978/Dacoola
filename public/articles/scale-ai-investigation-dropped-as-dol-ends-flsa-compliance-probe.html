Scale AI Investigation Dropped as DOL Ends FLSA Compliance Probe

The U.S. Department of Labor (DOL) has terminated its investigation into Scale AI’s compliance with the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), according to a source familiar with the matter. The probe, first reported by TechCrunch in March, examined allegations of worker misclassification and unpaid wages. Scale AI, valued at $13.8 billion, relies on contractors for critical AI labeling tasks, but former workers have sued, claiming they were denied employee benefits.

Key Developments in the Scale AI Probe

The DOL’s decision to drop the investigation comes amid shifting federal labor policies. On May 1, the agency announced it would no longer enforce a Biden-era rule that made classifying workers as independent contractors more difficult. This policy shift may have influenced the DOL’s approach to Scale AI’s case.

Scale AI’s spokesperson, Joe Osborne, confirmed the news, stating, “We’re pleased with this update. Creating new forms of work in AI is vital to America’s economic growth.” The company has faced lawsuits from former workers alleging underpayment and misclassification, but the DOL has not disclosed specific reasons for ending the probe.

Political and Regulatory Context

Scale AI has sought alignment with the Trump administration, with CEO Alexandr Wang attending Trump’s inauguration and advocating for AI dominance. Additionally, former Scale AI managing director Michael Kratsios now leads the White House’s Office of Science and Technology Policy, though this role does not oversee the DOL.

Meanwhile, Upwork and HireArt-Scale AI’s HR partners-also confirmed their investigations were closed. The broader implications for gig economy labor standards remain uncertain as regulatory priorities shift.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why was Scale AI under investigation?

The DOL investigated allegations that Scale AI misclassified workers as independent contractors, denying them benefits like sick leave and fair wages under the FLSA.

What does this mean for gig workers?

The DOL’s decision signals a potential relaxation of contractor classification rules, which could impact gig economy platforms reliant on freelance labor.

Has Scale AI faced other legal challenges?

Yes, former workers have sued Scale AI over alleged wage violations and misclassification, though these cases are separate from the DOL probe.
