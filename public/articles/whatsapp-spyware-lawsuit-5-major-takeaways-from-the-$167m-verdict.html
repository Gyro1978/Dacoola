WhatsApp Spyware Lawsuit: 5 Major Takeaways from the $167M Verdict

A jury ordered NSO Group to pay WhatsApp over $167 million in damages, concluding a five-year legal battle over the spyware maker’s exploitation of a vulnerability in the messaging app. The lawsuit, filed in 2019, accused NSO Group of hacking 1,400 WhatsApp users through a zero-click attack that required no interaction from targets. The trial exposed critical details about Pegasus spyware operations, including its use against a U.S. phone number and ongoing attacks even after litigation began.

Key Revelations from the Trial

Testimony revealed that NSO Group’s "WhatsApp Installation Server" sent malicious messages mimicking real calls, triggering spyware downloads. The attack only required a phone number, highlighting the sophistication of the zero-click exploit. NSO Group’s VP Tamir Gazneli admitted these methods, codenamed "Hummingbird," remained active until mid-2020-months after WhatsApp sued.

The trial also confirmed NSO Group tested Pegasus on a +1 U.S. number for the FBI, contradicting its claims of avoiding American targets. While the FBI declined to deploy the tool, the incident underscored potential risks to U.S. privacy.

Government Abuse and Operational Secrets

NSO Group’s CEO Yaron Shohat testified that Pegasus automatically selects hacking methods for government clients, prioritizing results over transparency. The company cut ties with 10 customers for misuse, including Mexico, Saudi Arabia, and Uzbekistan. Despite its controversial work, NSO Group operates openly from an Israeli headquarters shared with Apple-an ironic juxtaposition given Pegasus’ frequent targeting of iPhone users.
