Veritone Track: The AI Tracking Tool Helping Police Skirt Facial Recognition Bans

Police and federal agencies are using a controversial AI tracking tool called Veritone Track to bypass laws restricting facial recognition. The system identifies individuals based on attributes like body size, gender, clothing, and accessories, allowing law enforcement to track suspects even when facial recognition is banned. With 400 customers, including the Department of Justice and Homeland Security, the tool is raising privacy concerns similar to those surrounding facial recognition.

How Veritone Track Works

Veritone Track analyzes video footage to identify and follow individuals using non-biometric data such as clothing, hairstyle, and accessories. Unlike facial recognition, which requires clear facial images, Track can operate even when faces are obscured. The tool compiles timelines of a person’s movements across multiple video feeds, making it useful for criminal investigations. According to Veritone CEO Ryan Steelberg, the system was designed to "assist in identifying criminals or malicious behavior" where facial recognition is prohibited.

The AI tracking tool is already in use by federal agencies, including the DOJ, which adopted it in August 2024. Veritone claims the technology will soon be capable of real-time tracking on live video feeds, further expanding its surveillance capabilities.

Privacy and Civil Liberties Concerns

Civil rights organizations, including the ACLU, warn that Track poses significant privacy risks. Jay Stanley, a senior policy analyst at the ACLU, describes the tool as a "potentially authoritarian technology" that could be abused for mass surveillance. With federal agencies increasingly monitoring activists and immigrants, critics argue that Track could amplify overreach.

Nathan Wessler, an ACLU attorney, points out that the tool enables tracking on an unprecedented scale. "It creates a categorically new scale and nature of privacy invasion," he says. Unlike traditional surveillance, Track allows police to comb through vast amounts of video data that would otherwise be impractical to analyze manually.

Legal Loopholes and Future Implications

Facial recognition bans in cities like San Francisco and states like Maine have pushed law enforcement to adopt alternative tracking methods. Since Track relies on non-biometric data, it circumvents existing restrictions. However, critics argue that attributes like body size blur the line between biometric and non-biometric tracking.

Veritone has not disclosed whether Track is being used in jurisdictions where facial recognition is banned. With ongoing legal cases involving the tool, its long-term impact on privacy laws remains uncertain.
