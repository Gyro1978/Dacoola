WhatsApp Wins $167M in Landmark NSO Lawsuit Over Spyware Hack

A jury has ordered Israeli surveillance firm NSO Group to pay WhatsApp $167 million in punitive damages for exploiting a critical vulnerability in the messaging app to install its Pegasus spyware on users' devices. The verdict, delivered on Tuesday, marks a significant victory for Meta-owned WhatsApp and privacy advocates, as it sets a legal precedent against unauthorized surveillance. The jury also awarded $444 million in compensatory damages, bringing the total penalty to $611 million.

The case stems from a 2019 attack where NSO allegedly targeted 1,400 phones, including those of journalists, activists, and government officials, using a "clickless exploit" that infected devices without user interaction. WhatsApp's lawsuit accused NSO of creating accounts to initiate malicious calls that injected spyware into targeted phones.

Unpacking the Impact of the WhatsApp-NSO Verdict

This ruling is a major blow to NSO Group, which has long faced criticism for selling spyware to governments accused of human rights abuses. The verdict reinforces legal accountability for companies that develop and deploy invasive surveillance tools. It also strengthens protections for encrypted communication platforms like WhatsApp, which serve billions of users globally.

The exploit in question, known as a "zero-click" vulnerability, allowed NSO to install Pegasus spyware simply by placing a call—no user interaction was required. This technique bypassed WhatsApp’s end-to-end encryption, highlighting the risks of even the most secure platforms when exploited by sophisticated attackers.

Deeper Dive: How the NSO Exploit Worked

NSO allegedly created WhatsApp accounts in 2018 and later used them to initiate calls that exploited a flaw in the app’s voice-calling feature. These calls injected malicious code into the memory of targeted devices, connecting them to NSO-controlled servers. Citizen Lab, a cybersecurity research group, identified victims across 20 countries, including human rights defenders and diplomats.

Future Outlook: What’s Next for NSO and Spyware Regulation?

This ruling could spur stricter regulations on spyware vendors and embolden other tech companies to pursue legal action against surveillance firms. Governments may also face increased pressure to limit the use of such tools, particularly against activists and journalists.
